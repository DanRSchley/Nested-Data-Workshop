43 Binary and Count Outcomes in Experiments

43.1 The Problem: When Experimental Outcomes Aren’t Continuous

The experimental designs covered in this module — between-subjects, within-subjects, and mixed factorial — all assumed a continuous, roughly normally distributed outcome. But many experimental outcomes in psychology, marketing, and behavioral science are not continuous:

  • Behavioral choices: Did the participant donate? Choose the prosocial option? Click the ad?
  • Count behaviors: How many times did the participant help a confederate? How many words did they produce? How many items did they correctly recall?
  • Errors and successes: How many trials did the participant complete correctly?

Fitting a linear mixed model to a binary or count outcome is not just inefficient — it misspecifies the DGP. Predicted probabilities can exceed 1 or fall below 0. Residuals are structured (they depend on the mean), not random. Parameter estimates are biased.

Generalized Linear Mixed Models (GLMMs) extend the experimental mixed model framework to handle these outcome types correctly. The nesting structure (observations within participants, participants within conditions or groups) is handled identically to the continuous case. The difference is in the link function and distributional family.

43.2 Part 1: Binary Outcomes in Experiments

43.2.1 The Logistic Mixed Model for Experiments

For a binary outcome \(Y_{ij} \in \{0,1\}\) at trial \(i\) for participant \(j\), in a two-condition within-subjects design:

\[\text{logit}\bigl(P(Y_{ij} = 1)\bigr) = \underbrace{\beta_0}_{\substack{\text{average log-odds} \\ \text{at baseline}}} + \underbrace{u_{0j}}_{\substack{\text{person's tendency} \\ \text{to say yes}}} + \underbrace{\beta_1 \cdot \text{condition}_{ij}}_{\substack{\text{condition effect} \\ \text{on log-odds}}}\]

The random intercept \(u_{0j}\) captures stable individual differences in response tendency — some people are more likely to help, donate, or choose the prosocial option regardless of the manipulation. Failing to include the random intercept would ignore the clustering from repeated measurements, deflate standard errors, and produce overconfident inferences.

43.2.2 Example: Framing Effects on Donation Decisions

We simulate a within-subjects framing experiment. 80 participants each evaluate 16 donation appeals — half framed in gain terms (“saves 200 lives”), half in loss terms (“200 people will die”). The outcome is binary: did they decide to donate (1) or not (0)?

The true donation effect of loss framing is an odds ratio of 1.8 — loss-framed appeals have 80% higher odds of donation, on average. People also differ substantially in their baseline donation tendency (captured by the random intercept).

Code 
n_participants <- 80
n_trials_each  <- 8  # 8 gain-framed + 8 loss-framed trials per participant

# Person-level random intercepts (log-odds scale)
u0_person <- rnorm(n_participants, 0, 1.2)  # substantial individual differences

df_donate <- expand_grid(
  participant = 1:n_participants,
  framing     = c("gain", "loss"),
  trial       = 1:n_trials_each
) |>
  mutate(
    u0       = u0_person[participant],
    framing_dummy = as.integer(framing == "loss"),
    log_odds = -0.5 + u0 + 0.59 * framing_dummy,  # log(1.8) ≈ 0.59
    prob     = plogis(log_odds),
    donated  = rbinom(n(), 1, prob),
    participant = factor(participant),
    framing  = factor(framing, levels = c("gain", "loss"))
  )

# Observed donation rates by condition
df_donate |>
  group_by(framing) |>
  summarise(
    n_trials      = n(),
    donation_rate = mean(donated),
    .groups       = "drop"
  ) |>
  knitr::kable(digits = 3, caption = "Observed donation rates by framing condition")
Observed donation rates by framing condition
framing n_trials donation_rate
gain 640 0.384
loss 640 0.506

43.2.3 Fitting and Interpreting the Model

Code 
m_donate <- glmer(
  donated ~ framing + (1 | participant),
  data   = df_donate,
  family = binomial
)

# Results as odds ratios
broom.mixed::tidy(m_donate, effects = "fixed", exponentiate = TRUE, conf.int = TRUE) |>
  filter(term != "(Intercept)") |>
  select(term, estimate, conf.low, conf.high, p.value) |>
  knitr::kable(
    digits = 3,
    caption = "Framing effect as odds ratio. True OR = 1.8; loss framing increases donation odds."
  )
Framing effect as odds ratio. True OR = 1.8; loss framing increases donation odds.
term estimate conf.low conf.high p.value
framingloss 1.872 1.458 2.404 0

The odds ratio says: among participants with the same random intercept, the odds of donating in the loss-framing condition are estimate times the odds in the gain-framing condition.

43.2.4 Marginal Predicted Probabilities

Odds ratios are the correct internal currency of the logistic model, but they are unintuitive for most audiences. Report predicted probabilities:

Code 
preds_donate <- ggpredict(m_donate, terms = "framing", type = "fixed")

plot(preds_donate) +
  scale_color_manual(values = c("#1e3a5f", "#e63946")) +
  labs(
    title    = "Predicted donation probability by framing condition",
    subtitle = "Marginal predictions (averaging over individual differences)",
    x = "Framing condition",
    y = "Predicted probability of donating"
  ) +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 13)

43.2.5 The Nonlinearity of Marginal Effects

In logistic models, the effect of a predictor on the probability scale depends on where you are. An odds ratio of 1.8 translates to a larger probability increase when the baseline probability is 0.5 than when it is 0.1 or 0.9. This is a property of the logit link, not a problem.

A practical consequence: the same experimental manipulation can produce different probability effects for different sub-populations (people who generally give a lot vs. a little), even if the log-odds effect is identical. Reporting odds ratios alongside predicted probabilities at different baseline levels gives a complete picture.

Code 
# Show how OR translates to different probability differences at different baselines
baseline_logodds <- seq(-3, 3, by = 0.5)
effect_logodds   <- 0.59  # log(1.8)

tibble(
  baseline_logodds = baseline_logodds,
  baseline_prob    = plogis(baseline_logodds),
  treatment_prob   = plogis(baseline_logodds + effect_logodds),
  prob_difference  = plogis(baseline_logodds + effect_logodds) - plogis(baseline_logodds)
) |>
  ggplot(aes(x = baseline_prob, y = prob_difference)) +
  geom_line(linewidth = 1.3, color = "#1e3a5f") +
  geom_point(size = 2.5, color = "#1e3a5f") +
  geom_hline(yintercept = 0, linetype = "dashed") +
  labs(
    title    = "Same log-odds effect → different probability differences",
    subtitle = sprintf("Log-odds effect = %.2f (OR = 1.8). The probability difference peaks at p ≈ 0.5.", effect_logodds),
    x = "Baseline probability of outcome",
    y = "Probability difference due to treatment"
  ) +
  scale_x_continuous(labels = scales::percent_format()) +
  scale_y_continuous(labels = scales::percent_format()) +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 13)

43.3 Part 2: Count Outcomes in Experiments

43.3.1 The Poisson Mixed Model for Experiments

For count outcomes in a between-subjects design with \(j\) participants nested in condition \(g\):

\[\log\bigl(E[Y_{ij}]\bigr) = \underbrace{\beta_0}_{\substack{\text{log expected count} \\ \text{in control}}} + \underbrace{u_{0j}}_{\substack{\text{person's count rate} \\ \text{deviation}}} + \underbrace{\beta_1 \cdot \text{treatment}_j}_{\substack{\text{treatment effect} \\ \text{on log-count}}}\]

Exponentiating \(\beta_1\) gives the rate ratio — the multiplicative factor by which the count changes in the treatment condition relative to control.

43.3.2 Example: Prosocial Helping Counts in a Scarcity Experiment

We simulate a between-subjects experiment with 100 participants (50 per condition). Participants in the scarcity condition are told resources are scarce before an opportunity to help a confederate. The outcome is the number of helping behaviors observed in a structured lab task (counts: 0–10).

The true treatment effect is a rate ratio of 0.65 — scarcity reduces helping by 35%.

Code 
n_per_cond <- 50

df_help <- tibble(
  participant = 1:(n_per_cond * 2),
  condition   = rep(c("control", "scarcity"), each = n_per_cond)
) |>
  mutate(
    u0        = rnorm(n(), 0, 0.4),
    log_mu    = 1.8 + u0 + (-0.43) * as.integer(condition == "scarcity"),  # log(0.65)
    mu        = exp(log_mu),
    helping   = rpois(n(), mu),
    condition = factor(condition, levels = c("control", "scarcity"))
  )

# Summarize counts
df_help |>
  group_by(condition) |>
  summarise(
    mean_helping = mean(helping),
    sd_helping   = sd(helping),
    prop_zero    = mean(helping == 0),
    .groups      = "drop"
  ) |>
  knitr::kable(digits = 3, caption = "Helping count summary by condition")
Helping count summary by condition
condition mean_helping sd_helping prop_zero
control 5.50 3.052 0.02
scarcity 4.26 2.530 0.04

43.3.3 Fitting and Checking the Count Model

Code 
m_help <- glmmTMB(
  helping ~ condition + (1 | participant),
  data   = df_help,
  family = poisson
)

# Check overdispersion
pearson_resid <- residuals(m_help, type = "pearson")
dispersion    <- sum(pearson_resid^2) / df.residual(m_help)
cat("Dispersion ratio:", round(dispersion, 3), "\n")

43.3.4 When Poisson Is Not Enough: Overdispersion in Experiments

In within-subjects designs, participants complete multiple trials per condition, and individual differences generate more variance in counts than a Poisson model allows. This overdispersion is captured by switching to a negative binomial or adding observation-level random effects.

Code 
n_par_within  <- 60
n_trials_cond <- 6  # 6 trials per condition

df_help_within <- expand_grid(
  participant = 1:n_par_within,
  condition   = c("control", "scarcity"),
  trial       = 1:n_trials_cond
) |>
  mutate(
    u0        = rnorm(n_par_within, 0, 0.5)[participant],
    log_mu    = 1.5 + u0 - 0.43 * as.integer(condition == "scarcity"),
    mu        = exp(log_mu),
    # Add extra-Poisson variability (overdispersion)
    phi       = rgamma(n(), shape = 3, rate = 3),
    helping   = rpois(n(), mu * phi),
    participant = factor(participant),
    condition = factor(condition, levels = c("control", "scarcity"))
  )

m_nb_within <- glmmTMB(
  helping ~ condition + (1 | participant),
  data   = df_help_within,
  family = nbinom2
)

broom.mixed::tidy(m_nb_within, effects = "fixed", exponentiate = TRUE, conf.int = TRUE) |>
  filter(term != "(Intercept)") |>
  select(term, estimate, conf.low, conf.high, p.value) |>
  knitr::kable(
    digits = 3,
    caption = "Negative binomial mixed model: within-subjects scarcity on helping count."
  )
Negative binomial mixed model: within-subjects scarcity on helping count.
term estimate conf.low conf.high p.value
conditionscarcity 0.63 0.563 0.705 0

43.4 Part 3: Mixed Factorial Designs with Non-Normal Outcomes

Many experiments have both a between-subjects factor (e.g., treatment vs. control) and a within-subjects factor (e.g., pre vs. post, gain vs. loss framing). When outcomes are binary or counts, the GLMM handles this naturally through the random effects structure.

Code 
n_par_mf <- 100  # 50 per between-subjects condition
n_items   <- 8   # 4 items per within-subjects condition

df_mf <- expand_grid(
  participant  = 1:n_par_mf,
  ws_condition = c("gain", "loss"),
  item         = 1:4
) |>
  mutate(
    bs_condition = ifelse(participant <= 50, "low_stakes", "high_stakes"),
    u0           = rnorm(n_par_mf, 0, 0.9)[participant],
    log_odds     = -0.3
      + u0
      + 0.4 * (ws_condition == "loss")         # within-subjects framing effect
      + 0.7 * (bs_condition == "high_stakes")  # between-subjects stakes effect
      + 0.3 * (ws_condition == "loss") * (bs_condition == "high_stakes"),  # interaction
    prob         = plogis(log_odds),
    chose_risky  = rbinom(n(), 1, prob),
    participant  = factor(participant),
    ws_condition = factor(ws_condition, levels = c("gain", "loss")),
    bs_condition = factor(bs_condition, levels = c("low_stakes", "high_stakes"))
  )

m_mf <- glmer(
  chose_risky ~ ws_condition * bs_condition + (1 | participant),
  data   = df_mf,
  family = binomial
)

broom.mixed::tidy(m_mf, effects = "fixed", exponentiate = TRUE, conf.int = TRUE) |>
  select(term, estimate, conf.low, conf.high, p.value) |>
  knitr::kable(
    digits = 3,
    caption = "Mixed factorial binary outcome: framing × stakes on risky choice (odds ratios)."
  )
Mixed factorial binary outcome: framing × stakes on risky choice (odds ratios).
term estimate conf.low conf.high p.value
(Intercept) 0.652 0.438 0.969 0.035
ws_conditionloss 1.563 1.021 2.392 0.040
bs_conditionhigh_stakes 2.561 1.451 4.520 0.001
ws_conditionloss:bs_conditionhigh_stakes 1.125 0.604 2.097 0.710

The interaction term on the odds ratio scale tells you whether the framing effect differs by stakes condition — multiplicatively. A significant interaction (OR ≠ 1) means the framing effect is larger or smaller in the high-stakes condition. As with continuous outcomes, plotting predicted probabilities is essential for communicating the interaction to readers.

43.5 Assumptions and Diagnostics

WarningAssumption: Correct Link Function

The logit link assumes that the log-odds of the outcome are a linear function of predictors. This is generally robust and appropriate for most binary outcomes near the center of the probability range. If many outcomes are near 0 or 1 (rare or near-certain events), consider whether the probit or complementary log-log link fits better — though in practice, logit and probit rarely disagree meaningfully.

WarningAssumption: No Separation

Complete separation occurs when one predictor perfectly predicts the outcome (e.g., everyone in condition A donated, no one in condition B did). Maximum likelihood estimation fails in this case — the log-odds estimate goes to infinity. This typically signals a design problem (conditions too extreme, too few observations) rather than a modeling problem. Warning signs: very large standard errors, coefficients in the hundreds.

WarningAssumption: Correct Distributional Family for Counts

Start with Poisson. Check the dispersion ratio (Pearson \(\chi^2\) / df). If > 1.5, switch to negative binomial (glmmTMB(..., family = nbinom2)). If zeros are more prevalent than either model predicts, add a zero-inflation component (ziformula = ~1). The distributional family should match the DGP — it is a substantive assumption about how the data were generated.

WarningAssumption: Sufficient Cluster Size for GLMMs

Logistic mixed models can have convergence problems with small cluster sizes (few trials per participant) because the likelihood is flat near the random effects variance. A rule of thumb: at least 5–10 observations per cluster for reliable random intercept estimation. With fewer observations, consider Bayesian estimation (brms) which handles small-cluster GLMMs more stably.
