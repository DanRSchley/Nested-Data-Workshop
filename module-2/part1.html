16 Centering as Decomposition

16.1 One Variable, Two Mechanisms

When you measure “hours of sleep” across multiple days from multiple people, that variable carries two completely different stories at the same time:

  1. The within-person story: On days when this person sleeps more than they usually do, how does their mood change?
  2. The between-person story: Do people who sleep more overall have higher mood than people who sleep less?

These are different empirical questions. They may have different answers. They probably operate through different mechanisms — the within-person effect may reflect acute physiological recovery, while the between-person effect may reflect stable personality traits (conscientious people both sleep more and have better mood, not because sleep causes mood but because conscientiousness causes both).

Centering is not a cosmetic choice about where the intercept falls. It is a decision about which mechanism you are modeling — and whether you want to see both.

16.2 The Equations: From Conflation to Decomposition

16.2.1 The Conflated Model

When you include an uncentered (or grand-mean-centered) predictor in a random-effects model, a single coefficient \(\beta_1\) blends two mechanisms:

\[ \underbrace{Y_{ij}}_{\text{outcome}} = \underbrace{\gamma_{00}}_{\substack{\text{grand} \\ \text{mean}}} + \underbrace{\beta_1}_{\substack{\text{blended} \\ \text{slope}}} \underbrace{X_{ij}}_{\substack{\text{predictor}}} + \underbrace{u_{0j}}_{\substack{\text{person} \\ \text{RE}}} + \underbrace{\varepsilon_{ij}}_{\substack{\text{residual}}} \]

\(\beta_1\) absorbs variation from two sources simultaneously. Its value depends on which source dominates and how the two correlate.

16.2.2 The Decomposition

CMC separates the predictor into its two components before entering the model:

\[ \underbrace{X_{ij}}_{\substack{\text{observed} \\ \text{predictor}}} = \underbrace{(X_{ij} - \bar{X}_{j})}_{\substack{\text{within-unit} \\ \text{deviation}}} + \underbrace{\bar{X}_{j}}_{\substack{\text{unit mean} \\ \text{(between)}}} \]

Adding both as separate predictors gives the decomposed model. Notice how the single \(\beta_1\) from before splits into two:

\[ \underbrace{Y_{ij}}_{\text{outcome}} = \underbrace{\gamma_{00}}_{\substack{\text{grand} \\ \text{mean}}} + \underbrace{\beta_W}_{\substack{\text{within-unit} \\ \text{slope}}} \underbrace{(X_{ij} - \bar{X}_j)}_{\substack{\text{deviation from} \\ \text{own mean}}} + \underbrace{\beta_B}_{\substack{\text{between-unit} \\ \text{slope}}} \underbrace{\bar{X}_j}_{\substack{\text{unit's} \\ \text{average level}}} + \underbrace{u_{0j}}_{\substack{\text{person} \\ \text{RE}}} + \underbrace{\varepsilon_{ij}}_{\substack{\text{residual}}} \]

Now \(\beta_W\) answers the within-person question and \(\beta_B\) answers the between-person question. They are allowed to be completely different numbers with different interpretations.

NoteThe DGP Interpretation

The decomposed model is a hypothesis about the DGP: it asserts that two distinct mechanisms are at work, one operating at the within-unit level and one at the between-unit level. If \(\beta_W \neq \beta_B\), the DGP truly has two mechanisms. If \(\beta_W \approx \beta_B\), a single mechanism may span both levels — but you only know this after estimating the decomposed model and testing whether they differ.

16.3 Three Archetypal Patterns

The most important practical question is: how different are \(\beta_W\) and \(\beta_B\)?

16.3.1 Pattern 1: Convergent Effects — Exercise and Wellbeing

Both mechanisms point the same direction with similar magnitude. The within-person mechanism (acute endorphins, reduced rumination) and the between-person mechanism (fit people have better overall routines and self-efficacy) both increase wellbeing. True: \(\beta_W \approx \beta_B \approx 3\).

Code 
n_ppl <- 60; n_days <- 10

person_ex_avg <- rnorm(n_ppl, 45, 12)
person_wb_base <- 50 + 0.3 * person_ex_avg + rnorm(n_ppl, 0, 6)

df_ex <- expand_grid(person = 1:n_ppl, day = 1:n_days) |>
  mutate(
    exercise  = person_ex_avg[person] + rnorm(n(), 0, 8),
    wellbeing = person_wb_base[person] +
                3 * (exercise - person_ex_avg[person]) +
                rnorm(n(), 0, 5),
    person = factor(person)
  ) |>
  group_by(person) |>
  mutate(ex_mean = mean(exercise), ex_cmc = exercise - ex_mean) |>
  ungroup()

m_ex <- lmer(wellbeing ~ ex_cmc + ex_mean + (1 | person), data = df_ex)
broom.mixed::tidy(m_ex, effects = "fixed") |> filter(term != "(Intercept)") |>
  mutate(mechanism = c("Within-person", "Between-person")) |>
  select(mechanism, estimate, std.error, p.value) |>
  knitr::kable(digits = 3, caption = "Pattern 1: within ≈ between ≈ 3. One coherent mechanism at two levels.")
Pattern 1: within ≈ between ≈ 3. One coherent mechanism at two levels.
mechanism estimate std.error p.value
Within-person 2.996 0.030 0
Between-person 0.438 0.089 0

Here, the pooled model would give you a decent approximation — but you only know that after running the decomposition. The convergence itself is substantively informative: the same process operates at both timescales.

16.3.2 Pattern 2: Divergent Effects — Income and Life Satisfaction (Easterlin Paradox)

At the between-person level, wealthier people report substantially higher life satisfaction. But within individuals over time, income growth has a surprisingly modest effect — adaptation, shifting aspirations, and social comparison dampen the within-person response. True: \(\beta_W \approx 0.3\), \(\beta_B \approx 1.5\).

Code 
n_ppl2 <- 80; n_yrs <- 8

person_inc_avg  <- rnorm(n_ppl2, 50, 15)
person_sat_base <- 50 + 1.5 * person_inc_avg + rnorm(n_ppl2, 0, 8)

df_inc <- expand_grid(person = 1:n_ppl2, year = 1:n_yrs) |>
  mutate(
    income   = person_inc_avg[person] + rnorm(n(), 0, 6),
    life_sat = person_sat_base[person] +
               0.3 * (income - person_inc_avg[person]) + rnorm(n(), 0, 4),
    person   = factor(person)
  ) |>
  group_by(person) |>
  mutate(inc_mean = mean(income), inc_cmc = income - inc_mean) |>
  ungroup()

m_inc    <- lmer(life_sat ~ inc_cmc + inc_mean + (1 | person), data = df_inc)
m_pooled <- lmer(life_sat ~ income  + (1 | person), data = df_inc)

bind_rows(
  broom.mixed::tidy(m_pooled, effects = "fixed") |>
    filter(term == "income") |> mutate(model = "Pooled (blended)"),
  broom.mixed::tidy(m_inc, effects = "fixed") |>
    filter(term == "inc_cmc") |> mutate(model = "Within-person"),
  broom.mixed::tidy(m_inc, effects = "fixed") |>
    filter(term == "inc_mean") |> mutate(model = "Between-person")
) |>
  select(model, estimate, std.error, p.value) |>
  knitr::kable(digits = 3,
    caption = "Pattern 2: pooled ≈ 1.2, within ≈ 0.3, between ≈ 1.5. Pooled is wrong for both questions.")
Pattern 2: pooled ≈ 1.2, within ≈ 0.3, between ≈ 1.5. Pooled is wrong for both questions.
model estimate std.error p.value
Pooled (blended) 0.380 0.026 0
Within-person 0.336 0.027 0
Between-person 1.469 0.054 0

The pooled model gives ~1.2 — it overstates the within-person effect by 4× and understates the between-person effect. A policy conclusion based on this (“raising incomes will substantially improve wellbeing”) relies on the between-person relationship but must deliver on the within-person mechanism.

16.3.3 Pattern 3: Sign Reversal — Study Hours and Grades

Within a student, studying more than their own average helps their grades. But between programs, the harder programs have students who study more and earn lower grades — because the programs are harder, not because studying hurts. True: \(\beta_W \approx +2\), \(\beta_B \approx -3\).

Code 
n_students <- 200; n_programs <- 5
prog_difficulty <- c(2, 4, 6, 8, 10)
prog_study_avg  <- c(8, 7, 6, 5, 4)

df_study <- tibble(
  student = 1:n_students,
  program = rep(1:n_programs, each = n_students / n_programs)
) |>
  mutate(
    study = prog_study_avg[program] + rnorm(n_students, 0, 0.8),
    grade = 80 - 3 * prog_difficulty[program] +
            2 * (study - prog_study_avg[program]) + rnorm(n_students, 0, 3),
    program = factor(program)
  ) |>
  group_by(program) |>
  mutate(study_mean = mean(study), study_cmc = study - study_mean) |>
  ungroup()

m_study_decomp <- lmer(grade ~ study_cmc + study_mean + (1 | program), data = df_study)
m_study_pooled <- lm(grade ~ study, data = df_study)

bind_rows(
  broom::tidy(m_study_pooled) |>
    filter(term == "study") |> mutate(model = "Pooled OLS"),
  broom.mixed::tidy(m_study_decomp, effects = "fixed") |>
    filter(term == "study_cmc") |> mutate(model = "Within-program"),
  broom.mixed::tidy(m_study_decomp, effects = "fixed") |>
    filter(term == "study_mean") |> mutate(model = "Between-program")
) |>
  select(model, estimate, std.error, p.value) |>
  knitr::kable(digits = 3,
    caption = "Pattern 3: pooled is negative; within is positive; between is strongly negative. Sign reversal!")
Pattern 3: pooled is negative; within is positive; between is strongly negative. Sign reversal!
model estimate std.error p.value
Pooled OLS 5.013 0.165 0
Within-program 1.783 0.274 0
Between-program 5.790 0.145 0

p1 <- ggplot(df_study, aes(x = study, y = grade)) +
  geom_point(aes(color = program), alpha = 0.4, size = 1.2) +
  geom_smooth(method = "lm", color = "black", linewidth = 1.5) +
  scale_color_brewer(palette = "Set1") +

p1 + p2

The pooled model says studying hurts — which is wrong. The decomposition reveals the truth: within every program, studying helps, but harder programs demand more studying and are harder by definition. Ignoring the nesting produces a Simpson’s Paradox (see Part 3).

16.4 The Sleep-Mood Example: Applying Decomposition

Code 
n_people <- 50; n_days2 <- 7

person_sleep_avg  <- rnorm(n_people, 7, 1)
person_mood_base  <- 40 + 5 * person_sleep_avg + rnorm(n_people, 0, 5)

df <- expand_grid(person = 1:n_people, day = 1:n_days2) |>
  mutate(
    sleep_raw = person_sleep_avg[person] + rnorm(n(), 0, 1),
    mood      = person_mood_base[person] +
                2 * (sleep_raw - person_sleep_avg[person]) + rnorm(n(), 0, 4),
    person    = factor(person)
  ) |>
  group_by(person) |>
  mutate(sleep_mean = mean(sleep_raw), sleep_cmc = sleep_raw - sleep_mean) |>
  ungroup() |>
  mutate(sleep_gmc = sleep_raw - mean(sleep_raw))

m_uncentered <- lmer(mood ~ sleep_raw  + (1 | person), data = df)
m_gmc        <- lmer(mood ~ sleep_gmc  + (1 | person), data = df)
m_cmc        <- lmer(mood ~ sleep_cmc + sleep_mean + (1 | person), data = df)

bind_rows(
  broom.mixed::tidy(m_uncentered, effects = "fixed") |>
    filter(term == "sleep_raw") |>
    mutate(model = "Uncentered", mechanism = "Blended"),
  broom.mixed::tidy(m_gmc, effects = "fixed") |>
    filter(term == "sleep_gmc") |>
    mutate(model = "GMC", mechanism = "Blended"),
  broom.mixed::tidy(m_cmc, effects = "fixed") |>
    filter(term == "sleep_cmc") |>
    mutate(model = "CMC", mechanism = "Within-person"),
  broom.mixed::tidy(m_cmc, effects = "fixed") |>
    filter(term == "sleep_mean") |>
    mutate(model = "CMC", mechanism = "Between-person")
) |>
  select(model, mechanism, estimate, std.error, p.value) |>
  knitr::kable(digits = 3,
    caption = "Sleep → mood: blended ≈ 4.0; within ≈ 2.0; between ≈ 5.0. Neither blended estimate is correct.")
Sleep → mood: blended ≈ 4.0; within ≈ 2.0; between ≈ 5.0. Neither blended estimate is correct.
model mechanism estimate std.error p.value
Uncentered Blended 2.385 0.250 0
GMC Blended 2.385 0.250 0
CMC Within-person 2.162 0.262 0
CMC Between-person 4.611 0.771 0

The uncentered and GMC models give ~4, which is wrong for both questions. CMC reveals: the within-person mechanism is modest (2 points per extra hour of sleep); the between-person correlation is much larger (5 points) because healthy lifestyle confounds it.

16.5 Decision Guide

TipWhich Centering to Use
Goal Centering What you get
Interpret the intercept at a meaningful value GMC Intercept = E[Y] when X = grand mean
Estimate the within-unit effect CMC \(\hat\beta_W\) — deviation from own mean
Separate within and between mechanisms CMC + unit mean \(\hat\beta_W\) and \(\hat\beta_B\)
Test if within = between CMC + unit mean Wald test on equality
Single-level data (no nesting) Either No within/between distinction

Default recommendation for nested data: Always use CMC with the unit mean included. Run it, check whether \(\hat\beta_W \approx \hat\beta_B\), and only then decide whether the pooled model is defensible.

WarningAssumption: No Conflation of Within and Between Effects

Including a predictor without CMC in a random-effects model implicitly assumes \(\beta_W = \beta_B\). When this is wrong — as in Patterns 2 and 3 above — both estimates are biased.

How to test: Fit the CMC model. Compare \(\hat\beta_W\) and \(\hat\beta_B\). A Wald test (see Part 2) tests the null \(H_0: \beta_W = \beta_B\).

The stakes: In the worst case (Pattern 3), conflation completely reverses the direction of the effect.
