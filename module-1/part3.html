11 Random Slopes

11.1 When Effects Vary Across Units

The random-intercept model assumes that the slope — the relationship between a predictor and the outcome — is identical across all units. Only the baseline level differs from unit to unit. This is often an unrealistic assumption.

Consider two research settings:

Longitudinal panel data. An employee well-being study tracks workers across weekly waves. The average effect of workload on well-being may be negative — heavier workloads are associated with lower well-being. But some employees are highly sensitive: a small increase in workload produces a steep drop in their scores. Others are remarkably resilient: their well-being stays high regardless of how demanding their job gets. The slope of workload → well-being varies across employees.

Within-subjects experiments. A scarcity priming study asks participants to complete decision tasks under either resource-scarcity or control conditions. On average, the scarcity condition reduces decision quality. But individual responses to the manipulation vary considerably — some participants are strongly affected, others barely register a difference. The treatment effect itself is heterogeneous across participants.

The random-slope model addresses this directly. Rather than fixing one slope for everyone, we treat each unit’s slope as a draw from a distribution, allowing us to estimate:

  • The average effect across units (the fixed slope, \(\gamma_{10}\))
  • The variability of that effect across units (the random slope variance, \(\sigma^2_{u_1}\))
  • The correlation between where units start and how strongly they respond (\(\rho_{u_0, u_1}\))

11.2 The Model

Level 1 (observations within units): \[Y_{ij} = \beta_{0j} + \beta_{1j} X_{ij} + \varepsilon_{ij}\]

Level 2 (units): \[\beta_{0j} = \gamma_{00} + u_{0j}\] \[\beta_{1j} = \gamma_{10} + u_{1j}\]

Combined form: \[Y_{ij} = \underbrace{\gamma_{00}}_{\text{grand mean}} + \underbrace{\gamma_{10} X_{ij}}_{\text{avg slope}} + \underbrace{u_{0j}}_{\text{person intercept}} + \underbrace{u_{1j} X_{ij}}_{\text{person slope deviation}} + \varepsilon_{ij}\]

The random effects are assumed to follow a bivariate normal distribution: \[\begin{pmatrix} u_{0j} \\ u_{1j} \end{pmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} \sigma^2_{u_0} & \sigma_{u_0 u_1} \\ \sigma_{u_0 u_1} & \sigma^2_{u_1} \end{pmatrix}\right)\]

What does \(u_{1j}\) mean in each context?

  • Longitudinal: \(u_{1j}\) is employee \(j\)’s deviation from the average workload sensitivity. A large negative \(u_{1j}\) means this employee is especially sensitive to workload demands — their well-being drops steeply when workload increases. A near-zero \(u_{1j}\) means workload has its typical effect on this person.

  • Experimental: \(u_{1j}\) is participant \(j\)’s deviation from the average scarcity effect on decision quality. A large positive or negative \(u_{1j}\) signals heterogeneous treatment response — the manipulation was much stronger or weaker for this individual than average.

11.3 Longitudinal Example — Employee Wellbeing and Workload

Design: 50 employees measured at 8 weekly time points. The predictor is weekly workload (centered at its grand mean of 50). The outcome is well-being (0–100 scale). Each employee has their own workload sensitivity — modeled as a random slope.

The simulation imposes a correlation of −0.3 between intercepts and slopes, so employees who start with high baseline well-being are somewhat less sensitive to workload fluctuations.

Code 
n_employees <- 50
n_waves     <- 8

# Correlated random intercepts and slopes: SD_int = 8, SD_slope = 1.5, rho = -0.3
Sigma_long <- matrix(c(64, -3.6, -3.6, 2.25), nrow = 2)
re_mat     <- MASS::mvrnorm(n_employees, mu = c(0, 0), Sigma = Sigma_long)

df_long <- expand_grid(
  employee = 1:n_employees,
  wave     = 1:n_waves
) |>
  mutate(
    workload   = rnorm(n(), mean = 50, sd = 10),
    workload_c = workload - 50,
    well_being = (55 + re_mat[employee, 1]) +
                 (-2 + re_mat[employee, 2]) * workload_c +
                 rnorm(n(), 0, 5),
    employee   = factor(employee)
  )

# Random intercept only
model_ri_long <- lmer(well_being ~ workload_c + (1 | employee),
                      data = df_long, REML = TRUE)

# Random intercept + slope
model_rs_long <- lmer(well_being ~ workload_c + (workload_c | employee),
                      data = df_long, REML = TRUE)

broom.mixed::tidy(model_rs_long, effects = "ran_pars") |>
  knitr::kable(digits = 3,
               caption = "Variance components: workload → well-being random slope model")

The grey lines reveal substantial variation in workload sensitivity. Most employees show the expected negative slope, but the steepness varies considerably. A few employees show near-flat or even slightly positive slopes — for them, workload may not be experienced as burdensome during this observation period.

Intercept-slope correlation: A negative correlation (as simulated here) means employees who start with high baseline well-being tend to show a weaker negative workload effect. High-well-being employees may have resources — social support, autonomy, resilience — that buffer against workload demands. Conversely, employees already struggling (low baseline) may be most vulnerable to workload spikes.

11.4 Experimental Example — Scarcity and Decision Quality

Design: 60 participants complete 12 decision trials each (6 in the scarcity condition, 6 in the control condition, counterbalanced). The predictor is condition (scarcity = 1, control = 0). The outcome is decision quality (0–100 scale). Each participant is allowed their own scarcity sensitivity.

The simulation imposes a positive correlation of 0.2 between intercepts and slopes: participants with higher baseline decision quality show a slightly stronger scarcity effect.

Code 
n_participants <- 60
n_trials_each  <- 6   # per condition

# Correlated intercepts and slopes: SD_int = 10, SD_slope = 3, rho = 0.2
Sigma_exp  <- matrix(c(100, 6, 6, 9), nrow = 2)
re_mat_exp <- MASS::mvrnorm(n_participants, mu = c(0, 0), Sigma = Sigma_exp)

df_exp <- expand_grid(
  participant = 1:n_participants,
  condition   = c(0, 1),
  trial       = 1:n_trials_each
) |>
  mutate(
    decision_quality = (65 + re_mat_exp[participant, 1]) +
                       (-5 + re_mat_exp[participant, 2]) * condition +
                       rnorm(n(), 0, 6),
    participant     = factor(participant),
    condition_label = factor(condition, labels = c("Control", "Scarcity"))
  )

# Random intercept only
model_ri_exp <- lmer(decision_quality ~ condition + (1 | participant),
                     data = df_exp, REML = TRUE)

# Random intercept + slope for condition
model_rs_exp <- lmer(decision_quality ~ condition + (condition | participant),
                     data = df_exp, REML = TRUE)

WarningKey Point: Random Slopes and Type I Error in Experiments

Barr, Levy, Scheepers, & Tily (2013) demonstrated that omitting random slopes for within-subjects conditions inflates Type I error rates substantially. The mechanism: if you do not model variation in the treatment effect across participants, that variation is absorbed into the residual — deflating standard errors and making the average effect appear more precise than it is.

The recommendation: In within-subjects designs, include random slopes for all within-subjects factors by default (the “maximal” random effects structure). The random slope is not merely a nuisance parameter — it is the treatment effect heterogeneity, which is often scientifically interesting in its own right.

11.6 The Intercept-Slope Correlation

The random-slope model estimates not just variance in intercepts and variance in slopes, but also their covariance — equivalently, the correlation \(\rho_{u_0, u_1}\).

This correlation is substantively meaningful in both contexts.

Longitudinal interpretation. A negative intercept-slope correlation means: employees with higher baseline well-being tend to show a weaker negative workload effect. This is a buffering pattern — high-well-being employees are more resilient to workload fluctuations than their struggling colleagues.

Experimental interpretation. A positive intercept-slope correlation means: participants with higher baseline decision quality (in the control condition) show a stronger drop under scarcity. One interpretation: the scarcity manipulation particularly disrupts the elaborate, careful strategies that high-ability participants rely on — a “more to lose” pattern.

Code 
p_corr_long <- ggplot(employee_lines, aes(x = intercept, y = slope)) +
  geom_point(size = 2.5, color = "#1e3a5f", alpha = 0.7) +
  geom_smooth(method = "lm", color = "#e63946", se = TRUE, linewidth = 1) +
  labs(
    title    = "Longitudinal",
    subtitle = "Negative: high-baseline employees less workload-sensitive",

    y        = "Slope (workload effect)"

  theme_minimal(base_size = 12)

11.7 The Zero-Correlation Parameterization

By default, (predictor | group) estimates random slope variance and the intercept-slope correlation. When the correlation is near zero, theoretically uninteresting, or causing convergence problems, the zero-correlation parameterization separates the two:

# Full: estimates intercept variance, slope variance, AND their correlation
lmer(well_being ~ workload_c + (workload_c | employee), data = df_long)

# Zero-correlation: estimates both variances, forces correlation to zero
lmer(well_being ~ workload_c + (1 | employee) + (0 + workload_c | employee), data = df_long)

Applied to the experimental data:

Code 
model_zcp_exp <- lmer(decision_quality ~ condition +
                        (1 | participant) + (0 + condition | participant),
                      data = df_exp, REML = FALSE)

# Staged LRT: (1) does the random slope variance matter?
#             (2) does the correlation additionally matter?
model_ri_only_ml <- lmer(decision_quality ~ condition + (1 | participant),
                         data = df_exp, REML = FALSE)
model_zcp_ml     <- lmer(decision_quality ~ condition +
                           (1 | participant) + (0 + condition | participant),
                         data = df_exp, REML = FALSE)
model_full_rs_ml <- lmer(decision_quality ~ condition + (condition | participant),
                         data = df_exp, REML = FALSE)

anova(model_ri_only_ml, model_zcp_ml, model_full_rs_ml)
npar AIC BIC logLik -2*log(L) Chisq Df Pr(>Chisq)
model_ri_only_ml 4 4891.152 4909.469 -2441.576 4883.152 NA NA NA
model_zcp_ml 5 4879.032 4901.928 -2434.516 4869.032 14.1199269 1 0.0001715
model_full_rs_ml 6 4881.018 4908.494 -2434.509 4869.018 0.0135058 1 0.9074825

The sequence tests: (1) does the random slope variance matter at all? (RI vs. ZCP) and (2) does the intercept-slope correlation additionally matter? (ZCP vs. full). This staged approach identifies the minimum sufficient random effects structure.

When to prefer zero-correlation parameterization:

Situation Recommendation
Small sample (< 20 units) ZCP; estimating \(\rho\) is unreliable
Model fails to converge Try ZCP as a first simplification step
Correlation is theoretically uninteresting ZCP avoids unnecessary parameters
Testing whether the correlation matters Compare ZCP vs. full with LRT

11.8 Assumptions of Random-Slope Models

WarningAssumption 1: Multivariate Normality of Random Effects

What it means: The random intercepts and slopes are jointly drawn from a bivariate normal distribution. Severe departures — banana-shaped clouds, truncation, discrete clusters — suggest the normality assumption is violated, and estimates may be biased.

How to check: Plot the BLUP estimates from ranef() as a scatterplot. A well-specified model produces an elliptical cloud centered at the origin.

Code 
re_df_exp <- as.data.frame(ranef(model_rs_exp)$participant)
names(re_df_exp) <- c("u0_intercept", "u1_slope")

ggplot(re_df_exp, aes(x = u0_intercept, y = u1_slope)) +
  geom_point(size = 2.5, color = "#457b9d", alpha = 0.7) +
  geom_hline(yintercept = 0, linetype = "dashed", color = "gray50") +
  geom_vline(xintercept = 0, linetype = "dashed", color = "gray50") +
  labs(
    title    = "BLUP random effects: experimental model",
    subtitle = "Elliptical cloud centered at (0,0) supports bivariate normality",
    x        = "Random intercept (u0j)",
    y        = "Random slope for condition (u1j)"
  ) +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 13)
WarningAssumption 2: Correct Random Effects Structure Is Specified

What it means: The model must include random slopes for every predictor whose effect varies meaningfully across units. Omitting a necessary random slope pushes that variability into the residual, deflating standard errors for the fixed effect and producing anti-conservatively small p-values.

How to check: Use likelihood ratio tests (LRT) comparing models with and without the random slope. Models must be fitted with REML = FALSE for valid LRT comparisons.

Code 
model_ri_long_ml <- lmer(well_being ~ workload_c + (1 | employee),
                         data = df_long, REML = FALSE)
model_rs_long_ml <- lmer(well_being ~ workload_c + (workload_c | employee),
                         data = df_long, REML = FALSE)

WarningAssumption 3: Convergence

What it means: Complex random-slope models — especially with multiple predictors or crossed random effects — frequently fail to converge. A convergence warning from lme4 signals the optimization algorithm did not find a stable solution. Estimates from non-converged models should not be trusted or reported.

How to check: Use check_convergence() from the performance package.

Code 
check_convergence(model_rs_long)
[1] TRUE
attr(,"gradient")
[1] 5.426414e-06
Code 
check_convergence(model_rs_exp)
[1] TRUE
attr(,"gradient")
[1] 3.222788e-07

What to do if convergence fails:

  • Center and scale continuous predictors
  • Remove the intercept-slope correlation (see zero-correlation parameterization above)
  • Try alternative optimizers: control = lmerControl(optimizer = "bobyqa")
  • Simplify the random effects structure if the LRT supports it (non-significant random slope variance)
  • Consider Bayesian estimation via brms, which handles complex random-effects structures more robustly

11.9 Model Comparison — Do You Need Random Slopes?

Add random slopes when:

  1. Theory predicts heterogeneous effects. In organizational research, employee-level differences in workload sensitivity are plausible and interesting. In experiments, individual differences in response to priming manipulations are well-documented.

  2. The LRT supports them. The model with random slopes fits significantly better than the model without (at REML = FALSE).

  3. The design mandates them. In within-subjects experimental designs, random slopes for within-subjects conditions are structurally required to account for heterogeneous treatment effects. Omitting them is not conservative — it inflates Type I error (Barr et al., 2013).

  4. Sample size is adequate. Estimating a random slope requires sufficient variation in the predictor within each unit. Longitudinally: each employee must have multiple waves with varying workload. Experimentally: each participant must complete trials in multiple conditions.

Code 
bind_rows(
  broom.mixed::glance(model_ri_long_ml) |> mutate(model = "RI only",    setting = "Longitudinal"),
  broom.mixed::glance(model_rs_long_ml) |> mutate(model = "RI + Slope", setting = "Longitudinal"),
  broom.mixed::glance(model_ri_exp_ml)  |> mutate(model = "RI only",    setting = "Experimental"),
  broom.mixed::glance(model_rs_exp_ml)  |> mutate(model = "RI + Slope", setting = "Experimental")
) |>
  select(setting, model, AIC, BIC, logLik) |>
  knitr::kable(digits = 1,
               caption = "Model fit: random intercept vs. random intercept + slope")
