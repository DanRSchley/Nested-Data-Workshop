7  Overview

Module 1: The Mixed-Effects Framework

7.1 The Toolkit

Module 0 established that your data is almost always nested and that ignoring nesting inflates Type I error, shrinks effective sample sizes, and obscures the true data-generating process. Now we build the tools to do something about it.

The mixed-effects model is the central workhorse of nested data analysis. It is called “mixed” because it combines two types of effects:

  • Fixed effects — parameters that apply to the entire population. The average relationship between sleep and mood. The overall effect of a treatment.
  • Random effects — parameters that vary across units (people, firms, classrooms) and are treated as drawn from a distribution rather than estimated individually.

This combination lets the model do something powerful: borrow strength across units. A student who was absent for half the year still contributes information about their classroom. A person who completed only 2 days of a diary still informs estimates of the group average. The model uses all available information, weighted by how much each unit tells us.

7.2 How This Module is Organized

The module builds in steps, each adding one layer of complexity while being explicit about what assumption is being made and what assumption is being relaxed.

Part 1: Fixed Effects introduces the fixed-effects estimator — the simplest approach to handling nesting. It is easy to understand and makes strong assumptions. We examine each assumption carefully and show when it fails.

Part 2: Random Intercepts introduces the random-intercept mixed model: the most common multilevel model in psychology. We show how it differs from fixed effects, what it assumes, and how to check those assumptions.

Part 3: Random Slopes adds the most important extension: allowing the relationship between a predictor and outcome to vary across units. We discuss when this matters and when it does not.

Part 4: Fixed vs. Random — When to Use Which addresses one of the most frequently asked questions in applied multilevel analysis. The answer involves the scientific question, the data structure, and several testable assumptions.

Part 5: Three-Level Structures extends the framework to three levels of nesting and introduces the challenge of variance decomposition across levels.

Part 6: Crossed Random Effects covers the case where observations belong to multiple grouping factors simultaneously — the structure that arises naturally in most psychological experiments.

7.3 Core Assumptions

Every model in this module makes assumptions. We will state them explicitly at each step. For now, here is the set of assumptions that span the entire module:

ImportantAssumptions of the Mixed-Effects Framework
  1. Linearity: The relationship between predictors and the outcome is linear (at each level)
  2. Normality of random effects: The random effects are drawn from a normal distribution
  3. Normality of residuals: Level-1 residuals are normally distributed
  4. Homoskedasticity: The variance of residuals is constant across units and predictor values
  5. Independence of residuals: Level-1 residuals are independent after conditioning on the random effects
  6. Independence of random effects from predictors: (for random effects models) The random effects are uncorrelated with the fixed-effect predictors

Not all of these apply to all models. Fixed-effects models, for instance, do not require Assumption 6. Understanding which assumptions apply to which model — and how to check each one — is the goal of this module.
