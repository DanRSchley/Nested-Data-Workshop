14 Crossed Random Effects

Pure hierarchies assume each lower-level unit belongs to exactly one higher-level unit. But many real data structures — in both experimental psychology and longitudinal panel research — have units that belong to multiple grouping factors simultaneously. This chapter covers two major instances of crossed random effects:

  1. Within-subjects experiments: participants × stimuli (the classic psycholinguistics problem)
  2. Longitudinal panel data: firms × calendar years (the economy-wide shock problem)

Both cases share the same formal structure. Both produce badly wrong standard errors when one of the crossed grouping factors is omitted.

14.1 Part A: Crossed Random Effects in Experimental Designs

14.1.1 The Problem with Treating Stimuli as Fixed

A reaction time experiment: 40 participants each respond to 80 words. Each observation belongs to one participant AND one word. Participants are not nested within words. Words are not nested within participants. Both grouping factors are fully crossed.

If you only include a random effect for participants, you are assuming that all variance due to words is captured by the residual. But words differ systematically — some are rare and slow to process; others are frequent and easy. This word-level variance inflates your residuals and biases your standard errors.

Judd, Westfall, and Kenny (2012, 2017) showed that failing to include random effects for stimuli is one of the most widespread sources of Type I error inflation in social and cognitive psychology.

14.1.2 The Model

For observation \(y_{ps}\) from participant \(p\) responding to stimulus \(s\):

\[ Y_{ps} = \underbrace{\gamma_{00}}_{\text{grand mean}} + \underbrace{\beta_1 X_{ps}}_{\text{predictor}} + \underbrace{u_{0p}}_{\text{participant RE}} + \underbrace{w_{0s}}_{\text{stimulus RE}} + \underbrace{\varepsilon_{ps}}_{\text{residual}} \]

Where:

  • \(u_{0p} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2_u)\): participant random intercepts (some people are slower overall)
  • \(w_{0s} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2_w)\): stimulus random intercepts (some words are harder overall)
  • \(\varepsilon_{ps}\): trial-level residual noise
Code 
# Simulate a lexical decision task
n_participants <- 40
n_words        <- 80

participant_effects <- rnorm(n_participants, 0, 50)   # people differ in baseline RT
word_effects        <- rnorm(n_words, 0, 40)           # words differ in baseline RT
word_frequency      <- runif(n_words, 1, 10)           # word frequency predictor

df_ld <- expand_grid(
  participant = 1:n_participants,
  word        = 1:n_words
) |>
  mutate(
    participant_eff = participant_effects[participant],
    word_eff        = word_effects[word],
    frequency       = word_frequency[word],
    RT              = 550 - 15 * frequency +
                      participant_eff + word_eff +
                      rnorm(n(), 0, 30),
    participant     = factor(participant),
    word            = factor(word)
  )
Code 
# Without word random effects (wrong)
model_no_words <- lmer(RT ~ frequency + (1 | participant),
                       data = df_ld)

# With word random effects (correct: crossed)
model_crossed <- lmer(RT ~ frequency + (1 | participant) + (1 | word),
                      data = df_ld)

wrong_se   <- broom.mixed::tidy(model_no_words)[2, "std.error"][[1]]
correct_se <- broom.mixed::tidy(model_crossed)[2, "std.error"][[1]]

tibble(
  Model        = c("Without word RE", "With word RE (crossed)"),
  Frequency_SE = c(wrong_se, correct_se)

Model Frequency_SE
Without word RE 0.319
With word RE (crossed) 1.591

When word-level variance is ignored, the standard error for frequency is smaller — the model treats word-level variance as residual noise, artificially deflating uncertainty. This produces anti-conservative inference: p-values are too small.

14.1.4 The Assumptions of Crossed Random-Effects Models

WarningAssumption 1: Independence of Participant and Stimulus Random Effects

What it means: The random effect for participant \(p\) and the random effect for word \(w\) are assumed to be independent. Person 7 having an unusually fast RT is not related to whether word 42 is unusually easy.

How to check: In a balanced fully-crossed design, this is usually reasonable. In unbalanced designs (not all participants see all stimuli), check whether missingness is systematic.

WarningAssumption 2: Random Effects are Independent of the Predictor

What it means: The random intercepts for participants and words should be uncorrelated with the predictors. Word frequency should not predict which words were seen by which participants — in a balanced design, it cannot.

Why this is usually satisfied in experiments: In a fully-crossed, counterbalanced design, every participant sees every word, so assignment is orthogonal to word characteristics. This is one reason well-designed experiments support random-effects modeling.

WarningAssumption 3: The Same Model Applies to All Participant–Stimulus Pairs

What it means: The frequency effect is assumed to be the same for all participants unless a random slope for frequency by participants is included. If some participants are more sensitive to frequency than others, a random slope is needed.

How to check: Use likelihood ratio tests and theoretical reasoning.

Code 
model_maximal <- lmer(RT ~ frequency + (frequency | participant) + (1 | word),
                      data    = df_ld,
                      control = lmerControl(optimizer = "bobyqa"))

anova(model_crossed, model_maximal)

14.1.5 The “Keep It Maximal” Debate

Barr et al. (2013) argued that you should always include the maximal random effects structure — every random slope that is theoretically justified by the design. This minimizes Type I error.

Bates et al. (2015) and Matuschek et al. (2017) pushed back, arguing that maximal models frequently fail to converge, and that parsimonious models often provide better estimates.

The current consensus: include random slopes for all within-unit manipulations (this is critical), but simplify the correlation structure or remove slopes not supported by the data if the model fails to converge.

This debate is covered in depth in Module 5.

14.2 Part B: Crossed Random Effects in Longitudinal Panel Data

14.2.1 Handling Year Effects in Firm-Year Panels

In a standard firm-year panel dataset, every firm is observed in the same calendar years. Years are not nested within firms — instead, years and firms are crossed. Each observation belongs simultaneously to one firm AND one year.

The data-generating process has two sources of clustering:

  1. Firm random effects: stable between-firm differences (some firms are always more productive — due to management quality, technology, or location)
  2. Year random effects: economy-wide shocks that affect all firms in a given year equally (interest rates, commodity prices, regulatory changes, recessions and booms)

Why this matters — if year random effects are ignored:

  • Year variance is absorbed into the residual
  • Standard errors for predictors are too small (anti-conservative)
  • Economy-wide shocks appear as noise when they are actually systematic, shared variance

14.2.2 The Model

For observation \(Y_{ft}\) from firm \(f\) in year \(t\):

\[ Y_{ft} = \underbrace{\gamma_{00}}_{\text{grand mean}} + \underbrace{\beta_1 \text{Investment}_{ft}}_{\text{predictor effect}} + \underbrace{u_{0f}}_{\text{firm RE}} + \underbrace{w_{0t}}_{\text{year RE}} + \underbrace{\varepsilon_{ft}}_{\text{residual}} \]

Where:

  • \(u_{0f} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2_{\text{firm}})\): firm random intercepts (persistent productivity differences)
  • \(w_{0t} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2_{\text{year}})\): year random intercepts (economy-wide shocks)
  • \(\varepsilon_{ft}\): firm-year idiosyncratic noise

The formal structure is identical to the lexical decision model — only the substantive labels change.

14.2.3 Simulation: 30 Firms × 15 Annual Waves

Code 
n_firms <- 30
n_years <- 15

firm_effects <- rnorm(n_firms, 0, 6)   # between-firm productivity variance
year_effects <- rnorm(n_years, 0, 4)   # economy-wide year shocks (not in model)

df_panel <- expand_grid(firm = 1:n_firms, year = 1:n_years) |>
  mutate(
    investment   = rnorm(n(), 50, 15),
    productivity = 40 + 0.3 * investment +
                   firm_effects[firm] +
                   year_effects[year] +
                   rnorm(n(), 0, 5),
    firm     = factor(firm),
    year_fac = factor(year)
  )

Notice: year_effects — the year-level economy-wide variation — is not included as a predictor in any model below. It is the hidden source of year-level clustering that the year random effect must absorb.

14.2.4 Fitting Three Models

Code 
# Model 1: Wrong — firm RE only; year variance goes into residual
m_firm_only <- lmer(productivity ~ investment + (1 | firm),
                    data = df_panel)

# Model 2: Correct — crossed RE for firm and year
m_crossed   <- lmer(productivity ~ investment + (1 | firm) + (1 | year_fac),
                    data = df_panel)

# Model 3: Conservative alternative — year fixed effects (year as dummy variables)
m_year_fe   <- lmer(productivity ~ investment + (1 | firm) + year_fac,
                    data = df_panel)

14.2.7 Visualizing the Problem: Residual Heatmaps

A powerful diagnostic: plot model residuals as a heatmap over the firm × year grid. Under the wrong model, economy-wide shocks create vertical stripes — in a bad year, all firms have negative residuals simultaneously. Under the correct model, residuals are patternless.

Code 
df_diag <- df_panel |>
  mutate(
    resid_wrong   = residuals(m_firm_only),
    resid_correct = residuals(m_crossed)
  )

p_wrong <- ggplot(df_diag, aes(x = year_fac, y = firm, fill = resid_wrong)) +
  geom_tile() +
  scale_fill_gradient2(low = "#2166ac", mid = "white", high = "#d73027",
                       midpoint = 0, name = "Residual") +
  labs(title = "Wrong model (firm RE only)",
       subtitle = "Vertical stripes = year shocks pooled into residuals",
       x = "Year", y = "Firm") +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 12) +
  theme(axis.text.x = element_text(angle = 45, hjust = 1))

p_correct <- ggplot(df_diag, aes(x = year_fac, y = firm, fill = resid_correct)) +
  geom_tile() +
  scale_fill_gradient2(low = "#2166ac", mid = "white", high = "#d73027",
                       midpoint = 0, name = "Residual") +
  labs(title = "Correct model (firm + year RE)",
       subtitle = "No vertical stripes — year shocks absorbed by year RE",
       x = "Year", y = "Firm") +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 12) +
  theme(axis.text.x = element_text(angle = 45, hjust = 1))

p_wrong / p_correct

The vertical stripes in the wrong model are the visual signature of an omitted crossed random effect. Each stripe represents a year in which the economy was unusually good or bad — variance that is genuinely year-level but has been misattributed to the residual.

14.2.8 Year Fixed Effects vs. Year Random Effects

In panel data, analysts often absorb year effects using year dummy variables (year fixed effects) rather than a random intercept. The choice has a precise statistical interpretation:

Approach When to use Key assumption
Year random effects Years are a random sample from a broader population of time periods Normality of year effects; exchangeability
Year fixed effects These specific years are the complete population of interest No distributional assumption on year effects
Neither Years have no systematic shared component All year variance is idiosyncratic noise

Rule of thumb: With \(\geq 10\) years, random effects and fixed effects give similar coefficient estimates. With \(< 5\) years, fixed effects are more conservative and generally preferred because there is too little information to estimate the year-level variance reliably.

Connection to Module 1 Part 1 (Fixed Effects): Year dummies in a fixed-effects model ARE year fixed effects. The choice between fixed effects and random effects for the year dimension mirrors the general FE vs. RE choice from Part 4 — it depends on whether you want to generalize to years outside your sample.

14.2.9 The Assumptions of Crossed Panel Random-Effects Models

WarningAssumption: Year Effects are Exchangeable

What it means: The year random intercepts \(w_{0t}\) are assumed to be i.i.d. draws from \(\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2_{\text{year}})\). This implies no autocorrelation across years — a good year does not predict the next year will also be good.

When it fails: In the presence of multi-year macroeconomic trends (sustained expansions or recessions), year effects are correlated over time. The remedy is to include a linear time trend as a fixed effect, or to model AR(1) residual structure (covered in Module 4).

WarningAssumption: Firm and Year Effects are Independent

What it means: A firm being highly productive should not predict whether it was observed during good or bad years. In observational panels with attrition (firms entering and exiting), this can fail — high-performing firms may be more likely to survive through downturns.

How to check: Examine whether firm-level characteristics (size, industry, age) are systematically different across years of entry and exit. If firms drop out preferentially during bad years, year random effects will be estimated from a selected sample.

WarningAssumption: Crossed Effects are Independent

What it means: The firm random effect and year random effect are assumed to be independent. This fails if certain types of firms are disproportionately observed in certain years (non-random panel composition over time).

How to check: Does the distribution of firm types change substantially over years in your panel? For example, if a regulatory change in year 8 causes small firms to exit the panel, the remaining firms in years 9–15 will be a selected sample. The firm and year random effects are no longer independent.
